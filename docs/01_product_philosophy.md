# Purpose

This document defines the philosophical principles that guide every product decision.

Unlike feature requirements or technical specifications, these principles are intended to remain stable throughout the lifetime of the project.

Whenever a new feature is proposed, it should be evaluated against this document before implementation.

A feature that violates these principles should normally be rejected regardless of how attractive it appears.

---

# Core Philosophy

The project is built around one central belief:

> **The purpose of exercise is to preserve the freedom to live the life you want.**

Strength, mobility, flexibility and endurance are not goals by themselves.

They are tools that enable people to remain independent, capable and pain-free throughout life.

The application therefore optimizes for **quality of life**, not athletic performance.

---

# Principle 1 – Capacity over Performance

Most fitness applications reward performance.

Examples include:

- heavier weights
- more repetitions
- faster pace
- higher calories
- longer streaks

Those metrics are valuable for athletes.

They are not necessarily valuable for someone whose goal is healthy aging.

Instead, the application focuses on **capacity**.

Capacity represents what a person is capable of doing comfortably and consistently.

Examples:

- getting up from the floor
- carrying luggage
- climbing stairs
- hiking for several hours
- playing recreational basketball
- gardening
- lifting grandchildren
- remaining balanced on one foot

Improving capacity is considered more important than maximizing performance.

---

# Principle 2 – Consistency Wins

The most effective training program is not the hardest.

It is the one the user still enjoys five years later.

Therefore the application prefers:

- moderate daily sessions
- realistic progression
- flexible scheduling
- adaptive planning

instead of:

- maximal intensity
- punishment for missed workouts
- unrealistic challenges

The default session duration is approximately:

**20 minutes**

Users may configure this according to their lifestyle.

---

# Principle 3 – Train for Life

The application assumes that training should improve real life.

Every exercise should answer at least one practical question.

Examples:

"What daily activity becomes easier?"

Examples include:

- stronger grip → carrying groceries
- stronger legs → standing up easily
- stronger core → reduced back discomfort
- better balance → reduced fall risk
- improved mobility → reaching overhead shelves
- hip stability → healthier walking mechanics

Exercises that provide little functional benefit should generally receive lower priority.

---

# Principle 4 – Pain is Information

Pain is neither ignored nor feared.

Pain is a valuable signal.

The application distinguishes between:

- muscular effort
- muscular fatigue
- instability
- joint discomfort
- acute pain

Joint discomfort influences planning.

It should not simply terminate progression.

Instead, the planner attempts to:

- reduce unnecessary joint stress
- suggest alternatives
- modify intensity
- improve movement balance

without abandoning training entirely whenever possible.

---

# Principle 5 – Adaptation Instead of Fixed Programs

There is no perfect workout.

There is only the workout that is appropriate today.

Today's recommendation depends on:

- previous sessions
- recovery
- available time
- current pain
- recreational activities
- progression history
- long-term objectives

Two users should rarely receive exactly the same plan.

Even the same user may receive different sessions on different days.

---

# Principle 6 – Recovery is Training

Recovery is not considered inactivity.

Recovery is part of adaptation.

The planner therefore values:

- lighter sessions
- mobility work
- balance exercises
- breathing
- reduced volume

when appropriate.

Progress is measured over months and years rather than individual workouts.

---

# Principle 7 – Long-Term Thinking

The application encourages users to think in decades rather than weeks.

Instead of asking:

"How much stronger am I today?"

it asks:

"What physical abilities will I still have when I am seventy?"

This philosophy influences every progression decision.

Maintaining excellent movement quality is considered more valuable than chasing endless numerical increases.

---

# Principle 8 – Every Body is Different

Every user develops differently.

Examples:

- one person progresses rapidly in core strength
- another has excellent mobility
- another has previous shoulder injuries
- another has knee limitations

The application embraces asymmetrical progression.

Exercises evolve independently.

No attempt is made to normalize users against arbitrary standards.

---

# Principle 9 – Respect Existing Activities

The application recognizes that users already live active lives.

Examples:

- basketball
- hiking
- cycling
- gardening
- swimming
- manual work
- walking

These activities contribute positively to overall capacity.

The planner incorporates them instead of pretending they do not exist.

---

# Principle 10 – Teach, Don't Just Time

The application is a coach rather than a stopwatch.

Whenever possible it explains:

- why an exercise matters
- which muscles are involved
- which movement pattern it trains
- common mistakes
- safer alternatives
- expected progression

The user should gradually understand their own body better.

Education is considered part of the product.

---

# Principle 11 – Scientific Humility

Exercise science evolves.

The application avoids absolute claims.

Instead, recommendations should be based on:

- current evidence
- accepted coaching principles
- biomechanics
- practical experience

Whenever uncertainty exists, the application should remain transparent.

---

# Principle 12 – Accessibility

The project should remain usable by beginners.

Complexity should emerge only when necessary.

A first-time user should be able to start exercising within minutes.

Advanced users may explore detailed analytics later.

---

# Principle 13 – Privacy by Default

User data belongs to the user.

The application should:

- function offline
- require no account
- avoid unnecessary permissions
- avoid collecting personal data
- store information locally

Cloud synchronization should always remain optional.

---

# Principle 14 – Open and Extensible

The project is intended to become a platform rather than a fixed application.

New exercise packs should be installable without modifying application code.

Future examples include:

- rehabilitation packs
- yoga
- tai chi
- resistance bands
- kettlebells
- balance training
- sports-specific programs

The planner should operate on exercise metadata rather than hardcoded exercise names.

---

# Principle 15 – Motivation Through Competence

Users should feel encouraged rather than judged.

The application avoids:

- guilt
- punishment
- broken streak anxiety

Instead it emphasizes:

- progress
- understanding
- confidence
- consistency
- autonomy

Success is defined by sustainable improvement.

---

# Product Manifesto

We believe that movement is one of the greatest investments a person can make in their future.

We believe that exercise should support life rather than dominate it.

We believe that healthy aging is built through thousands of small, consistent decisions rather than extraordinary effort.

Our goal is not to create the strongest athletes.

Our goal is to help people remain capable, independent and confident throughout their lives.

Every feature should answer one simple question:

> **Will this help someone move well—not just today, but twenty years from now?**
